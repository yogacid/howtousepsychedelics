Is This For You?

Designed for
you if you...

Whether you are navigating anxiety or low mood, beginning or deepening a spiritual practice, or simply curious about psychedelics with enough self-awareness to want a careful approach — this is where to start.

  • Feel drawn to psychedelics as tools, teachers, or allies for self-understanding, healing, personal growth, or spiritual inquiry — and want to approach them thoughtfully.

  • Experience anxiety, emotional sensitivity, or periods of low mood and want a careful, contained approach rather than a generic protocol.

  • Want structure, reflection, and integration — not rigid protocols or vague spiritual inspiration without grounding.

  • Prefer learning within clear ethical and safety boundaries, and want to understand the risks as clearly as the possibilities.

  • Value community and mindful reflection — and have no interest in performative spirituality, productivity optimization, or biohacking.

This course is not for you if

You are looking for the largest possible dose, the most intense experience, or a shortcut to self-transformation. This course promises discernment, structure, and psychological safety — not outcomes.

Historical Context

A practice with deep roots.

Western Origins
Hofmann, Grof & the pioneers

Albert Hofmann — who first synthesized LSD — explored microdosing in his later years to enhance clarity, creativity, and connection to nature, viewing it not as a scientific curiosity but as a sacred tool for consciousness exploration. Stanislav Grof found that sub-perceptual doses could help people access deeper layers of the psyche while remaining emotionally grounded, facilitating healing and insight without the intensity of a full experience.

Indigenous Traditions
Low-dose practices in the Amazon and beyond

Long before the term "microdosing" emerged, Indigenous communities — particularly in the Amazon — used low doses of plant medicines as part of ongoing spiritual, relational, and medicinal practices. These doses served subtle purposes: enhancing connection to the natural world, maintaining energetic balance, and deepening participation in the living community of species. These traditions reflect a long-standing understanding of how to engage with psychoactive plants in non-visionary, relational ways.
